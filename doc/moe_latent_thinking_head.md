# MoE-Latent Thinking Head

The **MoE-Latent Thinking Head** (`MoELatentThinkingHead`) is the pinnacle of the Guacarpov Chess Engine's policy architecture. It merges the internal System-2 reasoning capabilities of Adaptive Computation Time (ACT) with the specialized, high-capacity routing of a Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE).

This architecture was designed to maximize theoretical capacity while maintaining a sub-10-million parameter active footprint, making it ideal for mobile deployment without sacrificing deep tactical vision.

---

## 1. The Architecture

The head operates in two distinct phases:

### Phase 1: The Pondering Loop (System-2)
When a raw board latent is received from the JEPA context encoder, it enters the **ACT Cell**. 
Instead of immediately trying to guess the best move, the cell recursively ponders the position by cross-attending over hallucinated future trajectories generated by the JEPA model.
- **Value-Guided Halting:** The loop queries the frozen JEPA Value Head at each step. If the board evaluation fluctuates (e.g., discovering a hidden fork), it keeps pondering. If the evaluation stabilizes, it halts early.
- **Gated Correction:** Using an LSTM-style Forget Gate, the cell can erase flawed initial thoughts if it discovers a better plan deeper in the loop.

### Phase 2: Sparse Expert Routing (System-1 Execution)
Once the ACT loop halts, it produces a refined, finalized `accumulated_thought` vector. 
Instead of mapping this vector directly to action logits (as the standard Latent Thinking Head does), it feeds the thought into a **Sparse MoE Router**.
- **The Gating Network:** Evaluates the thought vector and selects the Top-2 expert MLPs best suited for the situation (e.g., routing an endgame thought to the "Endgame Expert", or a king-hunt thought to the "Tactical Expert").
- **Expert Execution:** Only the two selected experts process the thought vector, combining their outputs to generate the final 4672-dimensional Leela action logits.

---

## 2. Why Combine Them?

### Separation of Concerns
In a standard neural network, the same layers must figure out *what* is happening on the board (the plan) and *how* to execute it (the precise move). 
By combining ACT and MoE, we enforce a strict separation of concerns:
1. **The Thinker (ACT):** Focuses entirely on understanding the position, spotting tactics, and formulating a plan.
2. **The Experts (MoE):** Focus entirely on executing specific categories of moves with high precision.

### The Unified Auxiliary Loss
Both architectures rely on penalties to function correctly:
- The ACT loop requires a **Ponder Penalty** to prevent it from thinking forever.
- The MoE router requires a **Load-Balancing Loss** to prevent it from sending every position to the same expert (expert collapse).

The `MoELatentThinkingHead` seamlessly unifies these into a single backpropagated signal:
```python
self._aux_loss = ponder_loss + self.moe_head.aux_loss
```
During SFT and RL training, PyTorch automatically minimizes this unified loss. The model simultaneously learns to think faster *and* distribute its thoughts evenly across its experts.

---

## 3. Performance & Efficiency

Despite having a high parameter count on disk (~1M for the Thinker + ~5.4M for the Experts), the network is **extremely sparse**. 

During inference (gameplay):
1. The ACT loop only runs for as many steps as strictly necessary.
2. The MoE Router only activates 2 out of the 8 available experts.

This means that for the vast majority of moves, the active parameter count evaluated by the CPU/NPU is less than **2.5 Million parameters**. It achieves the reasoning depth of a massive model while executing in milliseconds on constrained edge devices.
